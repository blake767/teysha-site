# What Breaks First When a Startup Moves Upmarket

Every founder I've talked to lately is chasing the same thing: bigger deals. More ACV per logo, less time spent closing $4K accounts one at a time. It's the right instinct — but almost nobody moving upmarket fails because the product can't do it. They fail because the *sales motion* was built for a different customer, and nobody rebuilt it before flipping the switch.

I've been on both sides of this. I helped lead a similar shift at Warmly, growing average deal size substantially while holding sales cycle length steady — and the thing that made it work wasn't a new pitch deck. It was rebuilding three specific parts of the motion that quietly stop working the moment your buyer changes.

Here's what actually breaks first, in the order it usually breaks.

## 1. Discovery stops being enough

At the low end, discovery is often a single call with the person who'll use the product. They feel the pain, they have a card, they can say yes. Thirty minutes, done.

Move upmarket and that call still happens — but it's no longer the whole story. There's now a buying committee: someone who *feels* the pain, someone who has to *validate* the solution technically, and someone who has to *approve* the spend. If your discovery process only maps to the first person, you'll build a beautiful case for someone who was never going to be the bottleneck.

The fix isn't a longer call. It's asking a different question earlier: *who else, besides you, has to agree this is worth doing?* Get names in the first conversation, not the fourth. Every deal that stalled for me in the early days of an upmarket push stalled because I found out about the real approver in week six instead of week one.

## 2. The demo stops matching how the buyer actually decides

A generic demo is fine when the buyer is the user and the decision is fast. It becomes a liability the moment there's a technical evaluator in the room who's being asked to underwrite risk, not just admire features.

What changed for me wasn't the demo script — it was what came *before* it. I stopped opening with the product and started opening by playing back exactly what discovery had uncovered, in the buyer's own words, before a single screen was shared. Then I only showed the parts of the product tied to that specific pain. Enterprise and mid-market buyers aren't more impressed by more features. They're more suspicious of a pitch that feels generic, because generic is what they've learned to associate with vendors who don't understand their business.

## 3. Pricing and packaging start fighting the deal instead of helping it

This is the one founders see coming least. The pricing model that worked at $4K ACV — simple, self-serve-adjacent, maybe a single tier — actively works against you at $40K. Bigger buyers expect to negotiate. They expect tiers that map to how their organization is actually structured. If your pricing page still looks like it's built for a five-person startup, it signals to a 500-person buyer that you might not be ready for them, regardless of what your product can actually do.

The fix here is usually not "raise prices." It's building a pricing structure that gives the champion something defensible to bring to their own approver — a business case anchored to cost-of-inaction, not a discount negotiation anchored to your list price.

## The pattern underneath all three

None of these are product problems. They're all versions of the same thing: **the motion was calibrated for a buyer who no longer exists**, and nobody rebuilt discovery, the demo, or pricing to match the new one. The product usually can support the bigger deal. The process almost never can, on day one.

The good news is this is fixable in weeks, not quarters — but only if it's treated as a deliberate rebuild instead of "let's just try selling to bigger companies and see what happens." That second approach is how six-month sales cycles happen by accident.
